## Month in Review

### 1. Claude Mythos Changes the Security Calculus Permanently

Anthropic's restricted Claude Mythos Preview was April's organizing story, and no other story came close. The model achieved a 72.4% success rate generating working zero-day exploits across all major operating systems and browsers — chaining a four-vulnerability browser exploit with a JIT heap spray to escape renderer and OS sandboxes, writing a 20-gadget ROP-chain RCE against FreeBSD's NFS server, and identifying a 27-year-old OpenBSD crash bug and a 17-year-old FreeBSD RCE without human guidance. Anthropic restricted access via [Project Glasswing](https://www.anthropic.com/glasswing) to 11 vetted partners including AWS, Apple, Cisco, CrowdStrike, Google, JPMorgan Chase, and Microsoft, with an explicit rationale: offensive capability, not safety policy, made general release unacceptable. Mozilla then used it to find [271 Firefox vulnerabilities in a single evaluation pass](https://blog.mozilla.org/en/privacy-security/ai-security-zero-day-vulnerabilities/) — twelve times the 22 bugs Claude Opus 4.6 found in an earlier scan — and patched all of them in Firefox 150. By month end, the White House was [drafting guidance to put Mythos in federal hands](https://www.axios.com/2026/04/29/trump-anthropic-pentagon-ai-executive-order-gov), Treasury Secretary Bessent and Fed Chair Powell had [summoned bank executives](https://techcrunch.com/2026/04/12/trump-officials-may-be-encouraging-banks-to-test-anthropics-mythos-model/) to encourage testing, and an unauthorized group had [accessed it via a compromised third-party contractor credential](https://techcrunch.com/2026/04/21/unauthorized-group-has-gained-access-to-anthropics-exclusive-cyber-tool-mythos-report-claims/) on the day of its announcement. Mythos did not begin as a security crisis. By April 30, it was a national policy debate.

### 2. Q1 2026 Funding Shatters All Records — $297 Billion

Global venture activity hit [$297 billion in Q1 2026](https://techcrunch.com/2026/04/01/startup-funding-shatters-all-records-in-q1/) — a 2.5x increase over any prior quarterly record. Two companies drove more than half the total: OpenAI's $122B round at an $852B valuation and Anthropic's $30B at $380B. The record understates the distortion. Prior to 2019, $297 billion would have exceeded a full calendar year of global VC. The concentration of capital at the frontier is not noise in the data — it is the signal.

### 3. Anthropic's Revenue, Compute, and Enterprise Scale

Anthropic disclosed its [annualized revenue at $30 billion](https://www.anthropic.com/news/google-broadcom-partnership-compute), up from $9 billion in Q4 2025, with over 1,000 enterprise customers each spending more than $1 million annually. Claude paid subscriptions more than doubled since January. The company simultaneously locked in 3.5 gigawatts of next-generation TPU capacity with Google and Broadcom beginning in 2027, then in late April secured a [separate $25 billion commitment from Amazon](https://www.anthropic.com/news/anthropic-amazon-compute) — $5 billion immediately at current valuation, up to $20 billion more tied to commercial milestones — for a total of 5 gigawatts of compute. The compute race is not theoretical; Anthropic is building the infrastructure for a training and inference capacity that did not exist anywhere in 2024.

### 4. TeamPCP / UNC6780 — The Month's Dominant Threat Actor

No threat actor operated more persistently across April than TeamPCP, formally designated UNC6780 by Google GTIG in week 15. The campaign began with the Trivy container scanner compromise in March, then expanded every week: [Cisco's internal development environment](https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/cisco-source-code-stolen-in-trivy-linked-dev-environment-breach/) yielded 300+ private GitHub repositories including AI product source code; the [European Commission cloud environment](https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/cert-eu-european-commission-hack-exposes-data-of-30-eu-entities/) yielded 92 GB from 29+ EU entities; LiteLLM and Mercor ($10B AI recruiting platform serving Anthropic, OpenAI, and Meta) followed. After a 26-day operational pause, TeamPCP [resumed in late April with three simultaneous attacks](https://thehackernews.com/2026/04/bitwarden-cli-compromised-in-ongoing.html) — Checkmarx KICS Docker images, the Bitwarden CLI npm package, xinference on PyPI, and a self-propagating npm worm (CanisterSprawl) that harvested 40+ credential categories and could pivot from npm to PyPI upon finding publish tokens. Mandiant quantified the broader campaign at 1,000+ compromised SaaS environments. No arrests, no public attribution beyond the UNC designation.

A model that led seven leaderboards Monday was challenged by two new releases before Friday. The cadence has compressed to the point where quarterly model release cycles are a historical artifact.

### 7. Meta Closes Closed — Muse Spark and the Manus Reversal

The following week, China's National Development and Reform Commission [ordered Meta and Manus to unwind their completed $2 billion acquisition](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/04/27/meta-manus-china-blocks-acquisition-ai-startup.html) — the first time Beijing reversed a foreign acquisition of a Chinese-founded AI company after the deal closed, citing AI technology leakage concerns. Meta had simultaneously acquired the talent (Scale AI, Alexandr Wang) and the closed-model strategy that makes Muse Spark possible, then discovered its ambitions for Chinese AI talent were geopolitically constrained in both directions.

### 8. Cohere Acquires Aleph Alpha — Sovereign AI Takes Shape

Cohere's [$20 billion merger with Germany's Aleph Alpha](https://techcrunch.com/2026/04/24/cohere-acquires-merges-with-german-based-startup-to-create-a-transatlantic-ai-powerhouse/), anchored by a $600 million commitment from the Schwarz Group and backed by the Canadian and German governments, pledged that all European public-sector data, model weights, and inference traffic would remain within STACKIT's sovereign perimeter. Combined with Anthropic's MOUs with Australia and Japan (the NEC partnership covering 30,000 employees), the Cohere deal represents governments beginning to treat AI infrastructure like telecommunications — as something that requires national sovereign guarantees, not just contract clauses.

### 9. DPRK and APT28 — Nation-State Supply Chain at Scale

North Korea's Sapphire Sleet [backdoored Axios npm versions 1.14.1 and 0.30.4](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/security/blog/2026/04/01/mitigating-the-axios-npm-supply-chain-compromise/) — present in roughly 80% of cloud environments — deploying a cross-platform RAT downloader targeting Windows, macOS, and Linux via a compromised maintainer account. Russia's APT28 (GRU Forest Blizzard) [compromised residential and SOHO routers to redirect DNS](https://krebsonsecurity.com/2026/04/russia-hacked-routers-to-steal-microsoft-office-tokens/), siphoning OAuth authentication tokens from Microsoft Office users across 18,000+ networks without deploying malware on target systems — 200+ organizations and 5,000 consumer devices in the collection network before FBI disruption. Chinese state-linked threat groups leveraged Anthropic AI systems for attack development and reconnaissance in [dozens of documented intrusions](https://therecord.media/chinese-hackers-anthropic-cyberattacks). The combination of supply chain targeting, OAuth token harvesting, and AI-assisted attack development in a single calendar month represents the operational maturity of nation-state cyber programs that no single mitigation framework addresses.

### 10. AI Developer Tools as the New Attack Surface

Flowise's CVSS 10.0 RCE ([CVE-2025-59528](https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/max-severity-flowise-rce-vulnerability-now-exploited-in-attacks/)), exploiting unsafe `Function()` evaluation in AI orchestration, with 15,000 exposed instances; Hashgraph Guardian repeating the identical pattern (CVSS 8.8); LiteLLM's pre-auth SQL injection ([CVE-2026-42208, CVSS 9.3](https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/hackers-are-exploiting-a-critical-litellm-pre-auth-sqli-flaw/)) exploited 36 hours after disclosure with production OpenAI, Anthropic, and AWS Bedrock credentials as the observed payload targets; OpenClaw accumulating 16 CVEs across the month via coordinated disclosure; and the Vercel breach tracing back to a Lumma Stealer infection at a third-party AI tool vendor. AI developer infrastructure has become a high-value attack surface before the security industry has developed appropriate review standards for it.

## Trend Lines

### 1. AI Capability as Dual-Use Weapon — Trajectory: Accelerating

Mythos began April as a product announcement and ended it as a policy dispute. The arc — controlled deployment via Project Glasswing → unauthorized third-party credential access → government push for federal access — compressed a theoretical dual-use debate into a lived operational sequence within thirty days. Bruce Schneier's [analysis](https://www.schneier.com/blog/archives/2026/04/what-anthropics-mythos-means-for-the-future-of-cybersecurity.html) framing voluntary restricted access as a non-durable containment strategy became more accurate with each passing week. The Cloud Security Alliance issued an [emergency brief](https://www.darkreading.com/cloud-security/csa-cisos-prepare-post-mythos-exploit-storm) warning CISOs that AI-assisted exploit weaponization windows would compress from months to hours. Mozilla's result — 271 Firefox vulnerabilities in a single pass — demonstrated that the acceleration is already here for defenders; the question is whether attacker-side replication lags by weeks or months, not years.

### 2. Capital Concentration at the Frontier — Trajectory: Self-Reinforcing

The flywheel is operating: more capital attracts enterprise customers ($1M+ annual contracts), which funds compute (5 GW committed), which trains more capable models, which generates the revenue that justifies the next capital raise. Anthropic went from $9B ARR in Q4 2025 to $30B in Q1 2026 — a 3.3x increase in one quarter. OpenAI's $852B valuation at the time of its Q1 raise exceeds the market cap of most Fortune 100 companies. The pattern is winner-takes-most; the distance between the two frontier labs and everyone else widened in April rather than narrowing.

### 3. Supply Chain as Primary Attack Surface — Trajectory: Entrenching

The shift from targeting organizations to targeting the tools everyone uses to build software accelerated throughout April. TeamPCP, Sapphire Sleet, the LiteLLM poisoning, and the Vercel/Context.ai infostealer chain all share the same logic: compromise a trusted dependency or vendor, wait, harvest credentials from downstream consumers. By month end, Trivy, Axios, LiteLLM, Bitwarden CLI, Checkmarx KICS, and VS Code extensions had all been targeted. Any development team that has not audited its dependency pins, container images, and CI/CD vendor trust chain since March 2026 should treat its pipeline as potentially compromised.

### 4. Sovereign AI Fracture — Trajectory: Hardening

April's sovereign AI story is not about one deal — it is about simultaneous signals that AI infrastructure is bifurcating along national lines. China reversed a completed acquisition. Two governments co-signed a $20B sovereign AI merger. Anthropic signed MOUs with Australia and formalized the NEC partnership in Japan. The White House drafted guidance to override OMB supply-chain risk designations for Mythos access. AI stack decisions that enterprise teams treated as vendor-neutral in 2024 are now geopolitically loaded — the question of which model you run and where it's hosted is becoming a compliance matter, not just an architectural one.

### 5. Open-Weight Pricing Pressure — Trajectory: Intensifying

DeepSeek V4-Pro shipped MIT-licensed open weights at $1.74/M input tokens, 49B active parameters from a 1.6T total, with a 1-million-token context window — trailing GPT-5.5 by an estimated 3 to 6 months in capability at a fraction of the API cost. The precedent from DeepSeek's January 2025 release held: frontier-class capability arrives in open-weight form within months of the closed-model frontier. Google's Apache 2.0 release of Gemini CLI and Gemma 4 (week 14) reinforces the pattern. Closed-model incumbents are not pricing against the open-weight alternatives available today; they are pricing against the open-weight alternatives that will exist in two quarters.

Watch for pricing adjustments from Anthropic and OpenAI, and for enterprise procurement teams to begin running open-weight deployments alongside closed-model pilots.

Organizations with multinational AI deployments face a near-term compliance decision about which jurisdictions require which model stack — and whether a single multi-cloud provider can satisfy all of them simultaneously.
